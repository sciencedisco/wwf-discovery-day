Showcase Guide

← Back to Overview

You've built something amazing—now it's time to share it with the world! This guide will walk you through deploying your dashboard to GitHub Pages so everyone can access it via a live URL.

Once deployed, you'll present your work to the group and celebrate your achievements together.

Deploy to GitHub Pages

GitHub Pages is a free hosting service that turns your repository into a live website. Follow these steps carefully to enable it for your project:

  1. Make sure your main HTML file is saved as index.html in the root of your repository (not in a subfolder).
  2. Commit all your changes in VS Code:
    • Open the Source Control panel (Ctrl+Shift+G or Cmd+Shift+G)
    • Stage all changes by clicking the + button
    • Write a commit message like "Add final dashboard"
    • Click the checkmark to commit
  3. Push your changes to GitHub:
    • Click "Sync Changes" or "Push" in the Source Control panel
    • This uploads your code to GitHub
  4. Go to your repository on GitHub.com in your web browser (the URL will look like github.com/[username]/[repo-name]).
  5. Click on the Settings tab at the top of the repository page (it's the gear icon on the far right).
  6. In the left sidebar, scroll down and click on Pages (under the "Code and automation" section).
  7. Under "Build and deployment", you'll see a section called Source. Click the dropdown that says "None" and select Deploy from a branch.
  8. Two new dropdowns will appear. Set them as follows:
    • Branch: Select main (or master if that's your default branch)
    • Folder: Select / (root)
  9. Click the Save button.
  10. Wait 1-2 minutes for GitHub to build and deploy your site. The page will refresh automatically.
  11. Once complete, a box at the top will show your live site URL: https://[username].github.io/[repo-name]/
  12. Click the "Visit site" button or copy the URL to test it in your browser!
Success!

Your dashboard is now live on the internet! Anyone with the URL can visit it. Share this link with your team, coaches, and fellow participants.

Making Updates:

If you make changes to your dashboard after deployment, just commit and push them to GitHub again. GitHub Pages will automatically rebuild and update your live site within a few minutes.

Troubleshooting

If your site isn't showing up or looks broken, try these fixes:

404 Error (Page Not Found):

Make sure your file is named exactly index.html (all lowercase) and is in the root folder, not in a subfolder. Check that you committed and pushed your changes to GitHub.

Charts or Data Not Loading:

If your charts work locally but not on GitHub Pages, check that your CSV file paths are correct. They should be relative paths like output/data.csv, not absolute paths like C:/Users/.../data.csv.

Site Still Shows Old Version:

Try a hard refresh in your browser (Ctrl+Shift+R or Cmd+Shift+R) to clear the cache. Sometimes browsers keep old versions temporarily.

Still Stuck?

Ask your coach for help! They can check your repository settings and help debug any issues. You can also ask Copilot: "My GitHub Pages site isn't working. What should I check?"

Presenting Your Dashboard

Now comes the exciting part—sharing your work with everyone! Here's how to prepare for your presentation:

  • Test your live site: Load it on a few different devices (laptop, phone, tablet) to make sure everything works
  • Prepare talking points: What story does your data tell? What insights did you discover?
  • Highlight the journey: Share interesting challenges you overcame or creative solutions you tried
  • Show your data sources: Explain where the data came from and why it matters
  • Practice together: Decide who will present what so everyone on your team gets to share
Presentation Tips:

Keep it concise (3-5 minutes). Show, don't just tell—actually interact with your dashboard during the presentation. Click on charts, scroll through sections, and demonstrate any interactive features you built.

Celebrate Your Achievement!

You did it! You've gone from raw data to a live, interactive dashboard that tells a meaningful conservation story. That's no small feat, especially if this was your first time coding or working with data visualization.

During the borrel, take time to explore what other teams created. See what different approaches people took, what chart types they chose, and how they told their stories. You might discover new ideas for future projects!

What You've Learned:

Working with data, designing user experiences, coding with AI assistance, collaborating in teams, and deploying to the web—these are valuable skills you can apply to future projects. Be proud of what you've accomplished today!
